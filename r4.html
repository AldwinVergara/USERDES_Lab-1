Information Architecture

Aldwin D. Vergara | October 17, 2020

For every paper, activity, and project that we do, the basic step that we should always take is to first plan out the idea and its structure, organization, and design. Same thing as to how websites are made, one should first understand and plan out what would the site look and how the sets of information are incorporated along the structure of the website. This organization and structuration of information with objects and design is what we call the Information Architecture — which is a key factor of a website development.


Among the people who plan out what they’re websites would look like and plan how the information in a site would be placed and work together, I am 100% not one of them. To be honest, as I’ve mentioned in my past reflection papers, I just put everything that comes up in my mind to my website that I think that is “cool” or “amazing”. Whenever I see something interesting or a feature from other sites that looked amazing such as movements of boxes as I scroll through the page, hover effects, image carousels, and moving or sticky menus, I would then immediately research how to implement them and work my way through the day to add them on my site. At the end of the day my site would look kind of amazing, but when viewed to see the bigger picture, it looks like a mind dump. But of course, I’m just a rookie for these things. And just like in games, in order for me and my skills to level up, I need XP (experience points) — and this is where my friend information architecture (IA) comes in. Upon the discussion, information architecture helps in organization of schemes or contents that allows access of information in various ways. IA is the structural design of shared information environments and science of organizing the contents of our websites. The lesson last time is very helpful for us since we are just starting to develop our own personal websites. From the discussion, we could easily pick general ideas and structures that we wanted to incorporate on the design and organization of our sites. The topic was interesting because I do like developing a website. This added knowledge would benefit me and my future plans for it gives me a wider understanding of website design. I’ve learned that there are a lot of ways to organize the schemes of our content. I find it interesting and amazing to see through the differences because I am also used and exposed to various websites that have different information architecture. For example, Twitter is a good example of a site with respect to date and Wikipedia is indeed a topic-centered site. For years of website development, the websites that I created always falls under ‘Organic’ in which there’s no organization of scheme and where I just put entry point pages. I’m not surprised with this fact, but I will make use of the knowledge I gain from IA to further improve on my future projects and provide a proper design and organization scheme. On the other hand, I am very much familiar and say knowledgeable on working my way through the types of navigation. I usually include navbars for most of my sites as a global navigation for the major parts of my site. I was able to work with local navigation such as sticky menus and buttons that let users move around the current section. And I also use contextual navigation and see also navigation for links for further information.


Overall, information architecture is highly concerned on the usability of the website and the experience of a user navigating the site. Websites are created for the users to use so we must organize the contents in a way lets the user easily navigate and understand the content. I am looking forward to organizing and coding my personal portfolio again and apply the proper information architecture. In this way, my site would have a professional look and structure and it will offer a more user-friendly web application.
